Paul Eccarius:

A boss once told me that group assignments are a microcosm reflection of the workplace – the good, the bad and the in-between. Taken through that lens, this assignment delivered in spades.

I really enjoyed working with the majority of the team - those that contributed were engaged, reliable and delivered work that made me try harder to make sure I was up to scratch. With a lot of things, we were very quickly on the same page, executing the same vision. I’m grateful to have been in such a great team. That said, that engagement was not universal and there were challenges in having everyone contribute – again, perhaps a reflection of what to expect beyond university.

With no clear leader role apparent from our personality type-testing results in Assignment 1, we elected to rotate leadership, each taking a turn at chairing meetings and taking minutes, with division of labour and decisions being made collectively. In hindsight, this approach was perhaps Utopian and I found myself falling into a bit more of a ‘driving’ role in keeping us on track with the deadline. I put this down more to my experience in my current job rather than a personality aptitude. For future assignments, I think electing a ‘team leader’ is probably a worthwhile alternative – I was worried about perhaps seeming to take too much control. I’ll be very interested to read the other’s thoughts around this.

These observations reflect my learning on groups – we each play a group role and exploiting these skills will likely yield better results than equality for equality’s sake. Overall however the group found it’s groove early and worked together really well. I’m looking forward to developing our Project in the coming assignments.

Key Group Takeaways

  • On the whole, the team reflections all recognise similar strengths and opportunities - this in itself is a more encouraging result than four wildly differing views on how the assignment was completed!
  • Everybody recognised the high quality of work delivered and mostly felt motivated to put their best effort forward.
  • Using the collaborative tools Microsoft Teams & GitHub proved to be mostly seamless - a point highlighted by the fact no two team members lived in the same city. Nour gets a special shoutout here for dialling in from Rabat in Morocco - a complete non-issue in the execution of the tasks and in particular, her conducting the interview with someone from the other side of the world.
  • While communication was high in context of the weekly/bi-weekly video calls, which everyone attended, an opportunity definitely presents itself for better use of Teams. We broke the different tasks into channels and this probably introduced unnecessary confusion into where comments and files belonged. As the team becomes more familiar and comfortable with GitHub as a collaborative platform, leaning more into this can potentially make this workflow clearer.
  • Attempting to execute the assignment ‘collectively’, rather than having a single ‘Team Leader’ probably proved to be the wrong choice in hindsight. This again ties into communication being an opportunity - increased clarity on group responsibilities beyond just producing work, and group expectations, will help iron out some of the consistency issues the team faced.
  • Another challenge was editing the work to read consistently across the assignment, as though it were produced by one team rather than five individuals. While most of the work submitted was of similar quality, accounting for individual differences will certainly require more attention from our group moving forward.

The encouraging part of this is that the main takeaways are all positive - the team worked comfortably together and produced quality work in a timely fashion.

Knowing our opportunities (Organisation, Communication and clarity of Roles and Expectations) moving forward will allow us to account for them early and set ourselves up for success in the next assignments.
